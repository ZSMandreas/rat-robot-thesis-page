Abstract

We study energy-efficient locomotion for a small quadruped rat robot performing a trot gait. As a learning-free reference we use a Central Pattern Generator (CPG) Planner and an open-loop Planner-simplified variant. We then train a reinforcement-learning controller (Ours) that tracks the same commanded velocity while minimising actuation effort.

At a matched forward speed of ~103.5 mm/s, the learned controller attains a cost of transport (COT) of 3.72, versus 5.69 for the Planner and 4.67 for the Planner-simplified baseline — roughly a 35% reduction relative to the planner. An energy-balance audit (closure validation) confirms the savings come from less negative/antagonistic work and lower contact and friction dissipation, rather than from measurement artefacts. This page collects the side-by-side locomotion clips, the per-component power and foot-trajectory animations, and the quantitative figures behind these results.

Approach

CPG planner baselines

The Planner generates a trot rhythm with a Central Pattern Generator and closes the loop on the robot state; the Planner-simplified runs the same rhythm open-loop. Both serve as learning-free references at the same commanded velocity and gait frequency.

Learned controller (Ours)

A reinforcement-learning policy is trained to track the commanded velocity while penalising mechanical effort. It discovers a trot that keeps propulsion useful and reduces antagonistic (negative) work and contact losses, yielding the lowest cost of transport at matched speed.

Energy-balance audit

To make the efficiency claim auditable, a per-substep energy decomposition (actuator work, joint damping, contact friction and normal dissipation) is closed against the change in kinetic/potential energy over a multi-stride steady window. The closure residual stays below 0.5%, attributing the savings to physically meaningful channels rather than artefacts.
